MIST

Multi-stage Transcranial Artifact Suppression Network for
Brain Ultrasound Localization Microscopy

Jihyeok Jung1, Hyungmin Lee1, Haedong Jeong2, Sua Bae1

1Department of Electronic Engineering, Sogang University
2Department of Art and Technology, Sogang University

Abstract

Background & Limitation: Ultrasound signals become blurred and distorted as they pass through the skull, making it difficult to clearly visualize cerebral vasculature. A major bottleneck in transcranial ultrasound research is the absence of ground-truth data; open-skull and transcranial image pairs cannot be acquired under identical conditions (e.g., due to tissue deformation or bubble shift).

Our Goal: To overcome the absence of ground-truth open-skull and transcranial observation pairs in clinical data, we generate structure-preserving pseudo pairs and train a model to restore transcranial images to open-skull quality.

MIST (Multi-stage Image Synthesis and Transcranial restoration): We propose a multi-stage network. First, we use CycleGAN with a Localization Loss to convert open-skull images into transcranial-style images, building a paired dataset while preserving microbubble spatial information. Second, we train a Uformer model to restore these synthetic transcranial images back to open-skull quality. This approach performs denoising, resolution enhancement, and PSF restoration.

Methodology

Stage 1: CycleGAN + Localization Loss

Since paired data is unavailable, we generate synthetic transcranial data from real open-skull images. Standard CycleGAN alone can distort structures and cause hallucinated artifacts. We introduce a Localization Loss to preserve microbubble dynamics and apply only the transcranial degradation pattern.

Stage 2: Uformer Restoration

Using the generated pseudo-pairs (Open-skull ↔ Synthetic Transcranial), we train a supervised restoration model (Uformer). This model learns to reverse the degradation, effectively removing skull-induced artifacts and enhancing vessel clarity.

Results

  • Quantitative Evaluation: MIST achieves significantly higher PSNR compared to baselines (Richardson-Lucy and CycleGAN-only). The Uformer restoration improves PSNR by 7-8 dB (up to 35.92 dB).
  • Qualitative Evaluation: Clearer vascular structures are observed. The network restores faint vessels and sharpens the image, overcoming the blur caused by the skull.
  • Simulation: We also validated performance using simulation data generated by the PROTEUS simulator (Musubi flow solver + Acoustic module), confirming the restoration capabilities.

Conclusion

MIST restores severely degraded transcranial ultrasound into clear cerebrovascular images without invasive craniotomy. It delivers vascular enhancement, improved image quality, and significant PSNR gains. Future work will expand quantitative and qualitative evaluations to verify physical fidelity.
